Subsidies help pastry chef buy in to a program she hopes will help others

By Brendan Meyer | Staff Writer

Published April 20, 2017

    Maricsa Trejo is healthy.

    She’s 26 and rarely schedules doctor appointments. She works as a part-time pastry chef, recently started her own business and currently lives with her boyfriend at his mother’s house.

    Money is tight.

    Yet two months ago, she bought her first health insurance plan on the exchange.

    “I’m getting older,” Trejo said. “Sometimes things happen, and you need it.”

    Trejo never had health insurance growing up. Her parents were poor. All doctor visits were paid out-of-pocket, so at an early age, the first-generation Mexican-American learned that if she had an ailment, it was best to just “ride it out.”

    Trejo was 20 when she first received health insurance. It was through her job, but even with the coverage, she hardly used it.

    That changed last year.

    Trejo was living and working in Oregon and had insurance through the bakery she worked for. She hurt her foot, and instead of riding it out, she went to the doctor, got X-rays and paid roughly $30 for the appointment.

    “That was the first time in my life I was like, ‘OK, this is how it feels to be taken care of by someone other than your parents,’” she said.

    Trejo moved back to her hometown of Dallas in November. Soon after, she began working at Small Brewpub in Oak Cliff as a part-time pastry chef. As she started her new company, La Casita Bakeshop, she decided that buying health insurance would be the responsible thing to do.

    She searched the marketplace and found a plan through Blue Cross Blue Shield of Texas. Due to her low income, she received a $204 monthly subsidy. In total, she pays $155 monthly premiums with no deductible.

    Trejo knows it’s a great deal. She couldn’t afford the plan without the subsidy but doesn’t anticipate using it much.

    Instead, she hopes that by buying in, she can help others.

    “That’s why I think health care is so important,” Trejo said. “I know I can’t do anything to help pay for somebody’s health care because I don’t have the money, but I know if I pay into it, that’s like helping other people.”

    Trejo did not vote in the past election. She was traveling outside of the country and never made the time, but she says she did not like either candidate.

    Instead, she preferred Vermont Sen. Bernie Sanders. Among other things, she was attracted to his platform on universal health care.

    Trejo is concerned about a repeal and replace. Now that she’s gone through the process of acquiring her own insurance, she wants to help her parents find their first health care plan and hopes that option remains available. Her father, who’s had multiple emergency room visits in the last five years, is still paying off medical bills.

    But she knows the ACA isn’t perfect — as evidenced by how affordable it was for her to find such a good plan.

    “It just doesn’t make any sense for someone like me to get something so good and then someone who can’t really afford it, but who needs it, not get that benefit [of a subsidy] at all,” Trejo said. “It just doesn’t make any sense.”
